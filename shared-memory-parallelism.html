Shared-Memory Parallelism: CPUs, GPUs, and In-Between

A course I developed around parallel programming on modern shared-memory systems, spanning CPUs, GPUs, and the practical space between them.

Course overview

I developed this course to teach students how to think about parallelism as a systems problem rather than as a collection of isolated APIs. The emphasis is on building intuition for modern multi-core and many-core machines, understanding how code maps to hardware, and learning how performance constraints emerge from memory, synchronization, and scheduling behavior.

The official Technion course page frames it around CPUs, GPUs, and the intermediate design space. In practice, that means a course that moves between classical shared-memory parallelism, accelerator-oriented programming, and performance-aware implementation.

Reading and course philosophy

On the official page, I point students to standard references such as Patterson and Hennessy, Fog, Chapman, and Wilkinson. My broader teaching philosophy is to combine principled systems understanding with actual programming practice, so that students leave with both conceptual grounding and working competence.
